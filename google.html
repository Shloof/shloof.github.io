UX Transformation

Google Calendar

Google Calendar is a powerful productivity tool, but the experience often feels rigid and impersonal.
In this project, we explored ways to make it more personal, flexible, and approachable without compromising its core functionality.
Role
UX Research & Interaction Design
Collaboration
Yael Barak — Shared research and interaction design

Goal

Reimagine Google Calendar as a more personal and intuitive experience through customization and simplified event editing.

The Challenges

Google Calendar is a powerful utility, but it lacks the tactile emotional connection that draws users to paper planners.
Emotional Gap
Introducing visual personalization without creating clutter that compromises information hierarchy.
Interaction Friction
Streamlining the fragmented editing flow to enable seamless updates without breaking user focus.
System Consistency
Integrating new features while staying aligned with Google’s Material Design language and existing user mental models.

User Research

While calendars became increasingly digital, paper planners continued to grow in popularity, with communities like #planneraddict gaining millions of posts on Instagram.
This reflected a desire for more personal and expressive planning experiences.
To better understand this shift, we mapped the strengths of both formats.
Paper remains appealing because handwriting feels personal and memorable, supporting stronger memory retention and emotional connection.
Survey
To better understand users' planning habits and motivations, we conducted a survey on how people use digital calendars in their daily lives.

Solutions

Event visibility

Current
Event visibility is currently limited, with most events appearing visually similar and customization restricted to color selection.
Suggested
To improve visibility and personalization, we introduced several enhancements:
  1. Larger titles for faster scanning
  2. Background images for distinction
  3. Quick access to links and attachments
  4. Custom icons for different event types

Quick editing

Current
Clicking an event opens the Quick Event Editing Panel, but editing still requires opening a separate window, interrupting the user's flow.
Suggested
To streamline editing, we turned the Quick Event Editing Panel into a directly editable workspace, allowing users to make changes instantly without leaving the calendar view.

Advanced editing

Current
Double-clicking an event opens the advanced editing mode with editable categories and tabs for details or "Find a time."
Suggested
To keep the calendar visible while editing, we moved advanced editing into a side panel.

Customized event

Current
Right clicking an event opens a dropdown with limited editing and customization options.
Suggested
  1. To improve customization, we expanded the dropdown menu with options for adding links and attachments.
  2. To keep the interaction lightweight, selecting the link option opens a popover for quickly adding or pasting a URL.

Customized calendar

First Iteration
Insights from the survey and research led us to introduce a customization layer, giving users more ownership over their calendar experience through visual themes and stickers.
We designed a set of preset themes combining background images, color palettes, and typography. Users can select a theme and further customize it to match their preferences.
After testing several background approaches, we found that combining background images with colorful events created too much visual noise. To maintain clarity, we moved toward a more focused and balanced solution.
Final Design
Since full background themes created too much visual noise, we shifted to a lighter approach, allowing users to customize colors, typography, stickers, and event images without overwhelming the interface.

Conclusions

This project pushed us to think carefully about the balance between usability, personalization, and visual clarity. Working through these challenges deepened our understanding of how people actually interact with digital calendars.
  1. Balancing personalization with visual clarity requires careful restraint.
  2. Editing directly within the calendar view improves workflow continuity.
  3. Familiar design patterns do not always fit Google Calendar workflow.
Looking ahead
Given more time, we would explore integrating tasks directly into the calendar view. Google Tasks and Google Calendar currently feel disconnected, and combining them could create a more unified daily planning experience.
